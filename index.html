About us

Here at SkillSwap, our aim is to connect those who know something with those looking to learn it. Instead of hiding what we do under inspiring statements, we are clear: we build technology to ease knowledge sharing and gather the most value possible from both participants.

We focus on making sure our investors get the best results by setting up a digital platform for sharing knowledge, with people providing the content, taking teaching risks and managing the quality, while we gather data, charge commissions and grow without additional costs.

SkillSwap is an example of platform capitalism because it turns individual skills into financial services, classrooms into payment-based agreements and protection for teachers into flexible job offers (Srnicek, 2017).
This is the age of learning

Where you pay for information and we show you how to make the most of it using Beer's (2019) principle of measuring everything people do.

Our offerings

SkillSwap functions as a multi-sided market, offering different services to three groups of customers, so as to produce valuable network effects by simply appearing to connect people.

For Skill Providers (Educators/Experts)

SkillSwap allows you to take your skills and make money through the system known as the 'hustle economy' (Ravenelle, 2019). The way we make your profile stand out most is through algorithms that favor revenue rather than your educational benefit.

Here's something we may not make very clear:

Your money covers all course design, educational background examination and quality control. The kind of entrepreneurship we highlight is based on following our algorithms and challenging peers in what Rosenblat (2018) describes as algorithmically-managed labor.

For Skill Seekers (Learners)

Our platform connects you to different skills which Srnicek classifies as an advertising platform that looks closely at your personal data to make tailored offers. What Yeung (2017) calls "hypernudging" describes our recommendation engine.

Something we don't emphasize:

The actual product is made up of your learning data. What Zuboff (2019) calls "behavioral surplus" consists of your preferences, questions and ways you use the content and it's collected to power the predictive products we sell to advertisers.

For Advertisers/Partners (Our Actual Primary Customers)

Follow the advice of Langley and Leyshon (2017) and invest in "platform capitalism," a business model meant to control the market by relying on venture capital, even though it projects itself as helping to connect users.

Our model helps us gather and monetize the attention of our users as described by Wu in 2016. By analyzing our behavior, we can do "hyper-targeting," a precise type of marketing described by Yeung (2017).

How it works

What Gillespie (2010) calls the "politics of platforms" explains our function: we appear impartial even as we exert substantial control over different interactions.

Step 1: Interface Design and Psychological Engineering

Due to our interface, those using the app believe they are learning directly from their peers, since our part is not visible. We refer to it as "choice architecture" using the method recommended by Thaler and Sunstein (2008).

The design that we propose is based on SchÃ¼ll's (2012) findings about using frequent but irregular incentives to hold people's attention longer.

Step 2: Algorithmic Management and Control

Unlike schools run by people, our algorithms are examples of "algorithmic governance", meaning fully automated decisions as Kitchin (2017) mentions.

What Bucher (2018) calls "programmed sociality" here is how our algorithm decides who interacts, mostly to improve our profits rather than focusing on students' learning.

Step 3: The Rating and Reputation System

Our framework highlights what Fourcade and Healy (2017) label as "classification situations," where people are organized by platform metrics.

Due to this, Rosenblat and Stark (2016) call it 'algorithmic management', where businesses measure and monitor instead of managing face-to-face.

Step 4: Network Effect Cultivation

Our approach depends completely on what Parker et al. (2016) call "network effects," which makes the platform more valuable as more users get involved.

In the same way Rochet and Tirole (2003) described, we offer reduced costs for learners to draw in many skill providers.

Step 5: Risk and Responsibility Displacement

The system we use strongly demonstrates what Hill (2015) describes as a shift in employment policies, greatly shifting risks from organizations to individuals.

The advantages for knowledge providers are limited to certification, managing how they are seen and content creation, whereas we keep what Pasquale (2015) called "algorithmic authority".

Impact

We show at SkillSwap that the idea of "the rise in the platform economy" (as explained by Kenney and Zysman in 2016) is transforming how knowledge is recognized and shared.

Economic "Empowerment"

Ravenelle (2019) lists our platform as an example of the "hustle economy" a situation where individuals are required to make money using their own knowledge and skills. The trend Standing (2011) observed is that the "precariat" made up of online knowledge workers who are often without financial stability, is becoming more prominent.

Educational "Democratization"

The platform we have shows what Williamson (2017) means by "platform education" the transformation of education by market technologies. Our approach, what Watters (2014) terms "ed-tech solutionism," suggests that technology can resolve educational challenges, even as it ignores the underlying factors that decide who takes advantage.

Community "Building"

SkillSwap demonstrates what van Dijck (2013) means by "platformed sociality" or social ties only formed through a commercial platform. As a result, this is what Floridi (2015) terms "onlife" the mixture of online and offline activities, where learning connections are frequently handled over commercial sites.

Investor relations

SkillSwap fits into what Kenney and Zysman (2016) explain as "platform capitalism": a way to make maximum profit at minimal direct expense.

Core Architecture: Algorithmic Control

Our platform follows the idea described by Gillespie (2010), known as "the politics of platforms," where neutral technology is used to exert power. We use algorithms referred to by Yeung (2017) as "hypernudge" to direct users to decisions that benefit the platform, while keeping it seem it is following their own decisions.

Sustainable and Expandable Business Model

The process by which we achieved profitability fits the platform business lifecycle described in Srnicek's (2017) study. It means what Langley and Leyshon (2017) call platform capitalism, where we keep finding new fields for platforms and growing our dependency on their infrastructure.

Future Vision

Our method for the future closely aligns with what Morozov (2019) terms "data extractivism," which means continual growth in areas where data is collected and valuable. We demonstrate what van Dijck et al. (2018) refer to as "platformization" the introduction of platform systems into fields of education that used to be free of markets.
